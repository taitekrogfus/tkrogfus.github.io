Hip Hop's Beginnings

Hip hop culture originated in the early 1970’s in the Bronx, NY. The music of this time heavily reflected social and racial issues of the late 20th century in America. Other factors included political discourse and a rapidly changing economy. Infrastructure in the area was suffering neglect and black and brown communities experienced the worsening of conditions in these neighborhoods, which brought a rise of crime, gang violence, poverty, and drug abuse. With businesses leaving the area, the youth sought entertainment and a place for self-expression on the streets.

Culture

Block parties started popping up in abandoned buildings, which led to the formation of hip hop culture. Artists began spray painting walls in an art form called graffiti and the main type of dancing that emerged from this time was called break dancing. Hip hop, or otherwise known as rap, became an outlet for music makers and audience alike.

Equipment

The 1980’s became a turning point in the industry as equipment became more readily available due to the more affordable price point. This equipment included synthesizers, samplers, and drum machines.

Golden Era

By the early 90’s Hip Hop had spread across the country and became the “Golden Era.” It was marked by its diversity, influence, stylistic innovation, and mainstream success. Some of the main players of this era were;

  • Wu Tang Clan
  • Rakim
  • A Tribe Called Quest
  • Beastie Boys

By the late 90’s these artists took the industry by storm. And by the end of the decade the genre had become mainstream and an integral part of society.

  • NWA
  • Dr. Dre
  • Tupac Shakur
  • Notorious B.I.G.
  • Nas
  • Jay Z
